About Xhuma

Xhuma is a response to a problem. Africa's internet sucks.

The table below is an excerpt from data published on Internet Penetration in Africa on Internet World Stats. These two lines of the table are all we need to see the problem:

Population (2019 Estimate) Internet Users (Mar-2019) Penetration (% population)
Africa 1 320 038 716 492 762 185 37,3%
Rest of the World 6 396 184 493 3 891 048 157 60,8%

extract from https://www.internetworldstats.com/stats1.htm

Africa’s internet problem is not new, nor surprising. There is a lack of infrastructure - very few people have active telephone connections and therefore are relient on cellphones. This leaves people using their mobile data, charged at roughly 10 Euro per gigabyte. Considering that 46% of South Africans bring in just less than 62 Euros a month, internet is an absolute luxury.

There are already many efforts to increase the penetration of Internet and smartphones in Africa, such as Project Loon by Alphabet.

There is obvious incentive to become the dominant service provider in the still untapped African Market. Facebook, for example, has been pairing up with service providers to offer free Facebook access to users. In some markets, they have implemented this so successfully that “Facebook” and “The Internet” are used interchangeably.

While this may sound like nothing more than a great business move on Facebook’s part, one has to consider the social, economic and political context at play. This gives the dominant provider such as Facebook or Google complete control over what users see and don't see. While we may assume no malintnet at these companies, the polarising effects of technology such as Facebook's feed algorithm have been demonstrated over and over again.

Xhuma as a Response

The idea behind Xhuma is to combine a number of existing technologies into a new package, aimed at low-end devices in developing markets.

The first and foremost is the idea of a mesh network, independent of service providers. This makes a lot of sense given the dense population in informal settlements and urban centres. Given the high data cost mentioned above, people are constantly looking for ways to stay in touch, for less. Also, it is worth considering that one needs a permenant address and government issue ID or passport for foreigners in order to purchase a SIM Card (On contract or prepaid). Given the number of people living in informal settlements or refugees without valid residency documentation, the barrier to entry to effortless communication is higher than it needs to be.

However, it is key that there is a seamless rollover to ISP based connections when they are more suitable (no peer around to connect to mesh or when on WiFi). This also ensures that sub-networks around the world can be linked together without needing a chain of devices between them.

Note: It is crucial that the ISP-based communication is still Peer to Peer (P2P).

If and When Xhuma has been established as a ubiquitous network throughout various markets, it would be feasible to exploit the network for more than communication. However, there are some limitations to consider:

  • Minimal processing power available per device
  • Limited devices with lengthy uptime

Given these limitations, it may be useful to introduce Xhuma-designed servers acting as peers on the network. One of these could serve a neighbourhood of people, or be used to mine for a cryptocurrency. They can also be used as information servers, providing localised information about weather, public transport timetables, public notices etc.

Even if there is not mass adoption of the network - or it's patchy in a certain area, one could walk up to the server unit (located at the end of streets?) and connect to it directly.

In short, Xhuma seeks to make use of existing technologies for developing markets. One can imagine the implications and uses of a widespread peer-based network, where everyone benefits from everybody else on the network.
